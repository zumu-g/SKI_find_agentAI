## 1. Understanding Your Property's True Value

Before you even speak to an estate agent, it pays to do your own homework. The more you understand about your property's market value, the better equipped you'll be to spot an agent who overvalues to win your instruction -- or one who undervalues to secure a quick sale.

### How to Research Comparable Sales

The single best way to understand what your home is worth is to look at what similar properties nearby have actually sold for -- not what they were listed at.

**HM Land Registry Price Paid Data**

The Land Registry records every residential property transaction in England and Wales. You can search for free at gov.uk/search-house-prices. This tells you the actual completion price, the date of sale, and whether it was a new build, semi-detached, terraced, and so on. Focus on sales within the last 12 months and within a quarter-mile radius of your property.

**Rightmove Sold Prices**

Rightmove's sold prices tool (rightmove.co.uk/house-prices) combines Land Registry data with the original listing photos and descriptions. This is invaluable because you can see the condition of comparable properties when they sold. A three-bedroom semi that sold for GBP 320,000 in immaculate condition tells a very different story from one that sold for the same price but needed a new roof.

**Zoopla Estimates**

Zoopla provides automated valuations based on an algorithm. These can be useful as a rough starting point but should never be taken as gospel. They cannot account for the condition of your specific property, recent improvements, or hyperlocal factors like being on a particularly quiet street.

**Key tip:** Create a spreadsheet of 5-10 comparable sales. Note the sold price, date, property type, number of bedrooms, condition (if visible from photos), and any obvious differences from your property. This gives you an evidence-based price range before agents walk through your door.

### Factors That Affect Valuation

Understanding what drives (and diminishes) property value helps you interpret agent valuations and prioritise improvements.

**Location factors:**
- School catchment areas (particularly for Ofsted Outstanding schools)
- Transport links and commute times
- Proximity to amenities (shops, parks, healthcare)
- Flood risk (check gov.uk/check-long-term-flood-risk)
- Planning applications nearby (check your local council's planning portal)
- Conservation area or listed building status
- Noise levels (road, rail, flight paths)

**Property-specific factors:**
- Size (total square footage matters more than bedroom count)
- Condition and age of key systems (boiler, roof, electrics)
- Energy efficiency (EPC rating)
- Natural light and orientation (south-facing gardens command a premium)
- Parking (off-street parking can add GBP 5,000-50,000 depending on location)
- Outdoor space (garden, balcony, terrace)
- Period features vs modern finishes (market dependent)

**Market timing factors:**
- Seasonal patterns (spring and early autumn typically see highest demand)
- Interest rate environment (higher rates reduce buyer budgets)
- Local supply and demand (how many similar properties are currently listed)
- Political or economic uncertainty

### Why You Should Get 3+ Valuations

Never instruct an agent based on a single valuation. Getting at least three valuations from different agents gives you:

- **A realistic price range.** If three agents independently suggest GBP 300,000-320,000, you can be fairly confident in that range. If one says GBP 280,000 and another says GBP 350,000, something is off.
- **Insight into agent quality.** The valuation visit is essentially an audition. You are assessing the agent as much as they are assessing your property.
- **Negotiating leverage on fees.** Knowing what multiple agents charge gives you a stronger position to negotiate.
- **A sense of marketing approach.** Each agent will present differently. Notice who asks the most questions about your property, who arrives prepared, and who follows up professionally.

**Warning: Beware of "buying the instruction."** Some agents deliberately overvalue properties to win your business, knowing they will push for a price reduction later. If one valuation is significantly above the others, ask the agent to justify it with specific comparable evidence.

## 2. Choosing the Right Estate Agent

Your choice of estate agent can mean a difference of tens of thousands of pounds in your final sale price, and months in how long it takes to sell. This is not a decision to take lightly.

### What to Look For

**Local expertise and track record**

The best agent for your property is not necessarily the biggest brand name. What matters is how well they perform in your specific area, at your price point, for your property type. An agent who dominates terraced house sales in one postcode may have no presence three miles away.

Look for:
- Number of properties sold in your area in the last 12 months
- Average time from listing to sale agreed
- Average percentage of asking price achieved
- How many similar properties they currently have listed
- Quality of their current listings (photography, descriptions, floorplans)

**Marketing quality**

Browse the agent's current listings on Rightmove and Zoopla. Is the photography professional? Are the descriptions detailed and compelling, or generic? Do they include floorplans? Virtual tours? These are the tools that will sell your home.

**Online presence and reviews**

Check Google Reviews, Trustpilot, and allAgents. Look for consistent themes rather than individual outliers. Every agent will have the occasional negative review; what matters is the pattern.

**Communication style**

During the valuation visit, notice how the agent communicates. Do they listen to your priorities, or just talk about themselves? Do they explain their reasoning clearly? The agent who communicates best with you will likely communicate best with potential buyers.

### Red Flags to Avoid

- **Significantly higher valuation than competitors** without strong evidence to support it
- **Pressure to sign immediately** or claims that they have a buyer waiting (this is almost always untrue)
- **Vague fee structures** or reluctance to put terms in writing
- **Tie-in periods longer than 12 weeks** (8-12 weeks is standard for sole agency)
- **No professional photography included** as standard
- **Poor-quality current listings** -- if their existing properties look bad, yours will too
- **Reluctance to provide references** from recent sellers
- **No accompanied viewings offered** (or only available at extra cost)

### Questions to Ask During Valuation Visits

Prepare these questions in advance and ask every agent the same ones so you can compare fairly:

1. How many properties have you sold within half a mile of here in the last 12 months?
2. What is your average time from listing to sale agreed?
3. What percentage of asking price do you typically achieve?
4. Can you show me three comparable sales that support your valuation?
5. What is your marketing package? (Photography, floorplan, portal listings, social media)
6. Who will conduct the viewings? Will it always be the same person?
7. How often will you update me, and how?
8. What is your fee structure, and what exactly is included?
9. What is the tie-in period and notice period?
10. What would your strategy be if we haven't received an offer within four weeks?
11. Do you have any properties currently on the market that have been listed for more than 12 weeks? If so, why?
12. Can I speak to two or three recent clients?

### Understanding Fee Structures

**Sole agency (typical: 0.75%-1.5% + VAT)**

One agent has the exclusive right to sell your property for an agreed period (usually 8-12 weeks). This is the most common arrangement and typically the cheapest. Most agents will negotiate on fees, particularly if you are selling a higher-value property.

**Multi-agency (typical: 2%-3% + VAT)**

Two or more agents compete to sell your property. You only pay the agent who finds the buyer, but the fee is significantly higher to compensate. This can be worth it in slow markets or for unusual properties, but for most sales, sole agency is better value.

**Joint sole agency (typical: 1.5%-2% + VAT)**

Two agents share the instruction and split the fee between them. This gives you wider coverage without the full cost of multi-agency.

**Online/hybrid agents (typical: fixed fee of GBP 999-4,999)**

Online agents charge a fixed fee, often payable upfront regardless of whether your property sells. The advantage is a lower total cost on higher-value properties. The disadvantage is that you typically get less hand-holding, fewer (or no) accompanied viewings, and reduced local expertise.

**No sale, no fee**

Most high-street agents operate on a "no sale, no fee" basis, meaning you only pay the commission if the property sells. Always confirm this in writing. Some online agents charge upfront regardless of outcome.

**Key tip:** Always negotiate. Agent fees are not fixed. If you are confident in your property and have multiple agents competing for your instruction, you have leverage. Even a 0.25% reduction on a GBP 350,000 sale saves you GBP 875 + VAT.

## 3. Preparing Your Home for Sale

The way you present your property can add (or lose) thousands of pounds. You do not need to spend a fortune, but you do need to be strategic.

### First Impressions: Kerb Appeal

Buyers form an opinion within seconds of arriving. The exterior of your home sets expectations for what is inside.

**Quick wins:**
- Clean the front door and polish or replace door furniture (handle, knocker, letterbox, house number)
- Repaint the front door in a smart, neutral colour
- Clean windows inside and out
- Weed and tidy the front garden or driveway
- Add a couple of planters with seasonal plants either side of the front door
- Ensure exterior lights work
- Clear and clean the path and driveway (a pressure washer can transform a tired driveway)
- Fix or repaint any peeling or damaged exterior paintwork
- Ensure wheelie bins are out of sight
- Repair or repaint the front fence or wall

### Decluttering and Staging Tips

**The golden rule:** Buyers need to imagine themselves living in your home. Anything that prevents that -- excessive personal items, clutter, bold decor choices -- works against you.

**Room by room:**

- **Entrance hall:** Clear coats, shoes, and bags. Ensure it feels spacious and welcoming. A mirror makes a narrow hallway feel wider.
- **Living room:** Remove excess furniture so the room feels larger. Aim for no more than two sofas and a coffee table. Remove personal photos from mantlepieces and shelves. Add a few tasteful accessories -- a throw, cushions, a coffee table book.
- **Kitchen:** Clear worktops completely, then put back only a kettle, a chopping board, and perhaps a fruit bowl. Clean behind and under appliances. Descale taps. Replace stained grout where possible.
- **Bedrooms:** Make beds with fresh, neutral bedding. Remove clothes horses and laundry baskets. Clear bedside tables. Ensure wardrobes are tidy (buyers will look inside).
- **Bathrooms:** Remove all products from the shower and bath edge. Display fresh, matching towels. Replace tired sealant. Fix dripping taps.
- **Spare room/home office:** If a room is doubling as storage, decide what it is and commit. A clearly defined guest bedroom or home office is worth more than a room full of boxes.

**Storage:** Buyers will open cupboards. Overstuffed storage suggests the house does not have enough space. Consider temporarily putting excess items in storage or with family.

**Deep cleaning:** Pay for a professional deep clean before your listing photos are taken. This is one of the highest-return investments you can make.

### Cost-Effective Improvements That Add Value

**Kitchen refresh (GBP 200-2,000):**
- Repaint or replace cabinet doors (a fraction of the cost of a new kitchen)
- Replace handles with modern alternatives
- Fit a new worktop if the existing one is badly damaged (laminate worktops start from GBP 200)
- Replace a dated splashback with a simple tiled or glass version

**Bathroom updates (GBP 100-1,500):**
- Regrout tiles
- Replace the toilet seat
- Fit a new shower head
- Replace a dated mirror and light fitting
- Add a heated towel rail (can be done for under GBP 100)

**Garden (GBP 100-1,000):**
- Mow the lawn and define the edges
- Add fresh bark or gravel to beds
- Pressure wash patios and paths
- Repair or stain fences and decking
- Add outdoor lighting or a small seating area to create lifestyle appeal

**General (GBP 50-500):**
- Repaint scuffed walls in neutral colours (Farrow & Ball "Cornforth White," "Skimming Stone," or "Elephant's Breath" are safe bets, or similar shades from Dulux at a lower price point)
- Replace dated light fittings
- Fix squeaky doors, sticking windows, and dripping taps
- Replace cracked or missing tiles
- Ensure all light bulbs work (LED warm white gives the best impression)

### Which Renovations DON'T Add Value

Be cautious with large-scale projects undertaken solely to increase sale price:

- **Loft conversions without building regulations sign-off:** An unconverted loft can be marketed as "potential." A poorly converted one is a liability.
- **Swimming pools:** In the UK climate, these are often seen as a maintenance burden.
- **Overly bespoke finishes:** Ultra-modern or very traditional finishes appeal to a narrow market. Neutral and well-executed always wins.
- **Converting the garage:** If parking is at a premium in your area, losing the garage may reduce your pool of buyers.
- **Over-improving for the street:** If every other house on the street is worth GBP 250,000, spending GBP 80,000 on an extension is unlikely to produce a GBP 330,000 sale. Buyers for that budget will look in a different area.

### Professional Photography Tips

Even if your agent provides photography, understanding what makes a good property photo helps you prepare:

- **Timing:** Photos should be taken on a bright day, ideally mid-morning or early afternoon for maximum natural light.
- **Preparation:** Every room should be camera-ready before the photographer arrives. Remove cars from the driveway. Open all curtains and blinds. Turn on all interior lights.
- **Wide angle:** Professional property photographers use wide-angle lenses. Ensure rooms look spacious by pushing furniture to the edges.
- **Hero shot:** Every property needs one standout image for the lead photo on Rightmove. For most properties, this is the exterior, the kitchen, or the living room.
- **Seasonal awareness:** If selling in winter, ensure the exterior photo is taken on a dry, bright day. Avoid photos with bare trees and dark skies.
- **Twilight shots:** For premium properties, "twilight" photography (taken at dusk with interior lights glowing) can dramatically increase click-through rates on portals.

## 4. Pricing Strategy

Pricing is arguably the most important decision in the entire selling process. Get it right and your property attracts strong interest and competitive offers. Get it wrong and it can languish on the market for months.

### The Psychology of Pricing

Buyers search property portals using price brackets. On Rightmove, a buyer searching for homes up to GBP 300,000 will never see your property if it is listed at GBP 310,000. Understanding these thresholds is crucial.

**Price brackets on Rightmove (commonly used):**
- GBP 200,000 / GBP 225,000 / GBP 250,000 / GBP 275,000 / GBP 300,000
- GBP 325,000 / GBP 350,000 / GBP 375,000 / GBP 400,000
- GBP 425,000 / GBP 450,000 / GBP 475,000 / GBP 500,000

If your property is worth around GBP 310,000, listing at GBP 300,000 puts you in the searches of every buyer up to GBP 300,000 AND every buyer above that figure. Listing at GBP 310,000 misses an entire bracket of buyers.

**The "just above" trap:** Listing at GBP 305,000 or GBP 302,000 signals to buyers that you are trying to squeeze into a higher bracket. It can feel off-putting and attract more aggressive negotiators.

### Offers Over vs Fixed Price vs Guide Price

**Asking price / Fixed price:** The standard approach in England and Wales. You set a price and expect offers near or at that level. "Offers in the region of" (OIRO) signals some flexibility.

**Offers over:** More common in Scotland but increasingly used in competitive English markets. Sets a baseline and invites buyers to bid above it. Works well in hot markets but can deter buyers who worry about being outbid.

**Guide price:** Often used for properties with uncertain value (unusual properties, renovation projects, or auction lots). Suggests a ballpark and invites offers. Can generate interest but may also attract timewasters if the guide is set too low.

### The Danger of Overpricing

This is worth emphasising because it is the single most common mistake sellers make, often encouraged by agents who overvalue to win the instruction.

**What happens when you overprice:**
1. Your property attracts fewer viewings because it is poor value compared to correctly priced competitors.
2. The buyers who can afford your asking price are comparing it with better properties.
3. After 4-6 weeks with low interest, you reduce the price.
4. The price reduction is visible on Rightmove (buyers can see "price history"). Buyers assume something is wrong.
5. Savvy buyers wait for further reductions, sensing desperation.
6. You eventually sell for less than you would have achieved if priced correctly from day one.

**Data consistently shows:** Properties that are priced correctly from the start sell faster and for a higher percentage of asking price than those that are reduced. A well-priced property generates competition; an overpriced property generates silence.

### When to Consider Price Reductions

If your property has been on the market for 4-6 weeks without a single offer, and you have had reasonable viewing numbers, the price is likely too high. If you have had very few viewings, the price (or marketing quality) is definitely the issue.

**Best practice for reductions:**
- Reduce by enough to make a difference (at least 5%)
- Time the reduction to coincide with a new marketing push
- Drop into a lower Rightmove search bracket if possible
- Ask your agent to re-list as "new" on portals where possible (some portals allow this)

## 5. Marketing Your Property

Even a beautifully presented, well-priced property will not sell if nobody sees it. Effective marketing is what separates a good agent from a mediocre one.

### What Good Marketing Looks Like

A well-marketed property has:
- Professional photography (minimum 15-20 images)
- A detailed, accurate floorplan
- A compelling written description (not just a list of rooms)
- Listings on all major portals
- Premium placement on at least one portal
- Social media promotion
- An EPC (Energy Performance Certificate) in place before listing

### Portal Listings

**Rightmove** is the UK's largest property portal with over 80% of buyer traffic. Listing on Rightmove is non-negotiable. Many agents offer "Featured" or "Premium" listing upgrades that place your property higher in search results and add additional photos to the listing card.

**Zoopla** is the second-largest portal and merged with Primelocation in 2023. Listing here is also essential for maximum exposure.

**OnTheMarket** is the third major portal. While smaller, it captures a meaningful segment of buyers, particularly at the premium end of the market.

**Key questions for your agent:**
- Which portals will my property appear on?
- Will you pay for a Featured or Premium listing? If not, can I pay for the upgrade?
- How quickly will the listing go live after photos are taken?

### Social Media Marketing

A growing number of buyers discover properties through social media, particularly Instagram and Facebook. Good agents use social media to:

- Share individual property videos and photo carousels
- Run targeted Facebook/Instagram ads to people looking to move to your area
- Showcase your property to their existing audience of active buyers
- Create "coming soon" teaser posts before the property goes live on portals

Ask your agent about their social media presence and what they plan to do specifically for your property.

### Floorplans and Virtual Tours

**Floorplans** are the second most important element of a listing after photos. Rightmove data shows that listings with floorplans receive 30% more interest. A good floorplan shows room dimensions, layout, and flow. Never list without one.

**Virtual tours** (Matterport, iStaging, or similar) allow buyers to "walk through" your property online. They are particularly valuable for:
- Out-of-area buyers who cannot easily visit
- Reducing time-wasting viewings from unserious buyers
- Properties with unusual layouts that are hard to understand from photos alone

### Open House vs Private Viewings

**Open houses** (also called open days) involve inviting all interested buyers to view during a set time window, typically a Saturday morning. The advantage is efficiency (all viewings done in one go) and creating a sense of competition as buyers see each other. The disadvantage is reduced attention for each buyer and potential security concerns.

**Private viewings** allow each buyer dedicated time with the agent. This is more time-consuming but allows the agent to tailor the tour to each buyer's priorities and build rapport. For higher-value properties, private viewings are almost always preferred.

**Accompanied vs unaccompanied viewings:** Ideally, your agent should accompany every viewing. An experienced agent can read buyer body language, handle objections in real time, and close towards an offer. If your agent sends buyers round on their own with a key, question whether you have the right agent.

## 6. The Sales Process

Once you accept an offer, the real work begins. The period between sale agreed and completion is where many sales fall through. Understanding the process helps you keep things on track.

### Understanding the Chain

A "chain" forms when your sale depends on your buyer selling their property, and their buyer selling theirs, and so on. The longer the chain, the higher the risk of collapse.

**Types of buyer (in order of attractiveness):**
1. **Cash buyer with no property to sell:** The gold standard. Quickest to complete, least likely to fall through.
2. **First-time buyer with mortgage approval:** No chain below them. Usually reliable but dependent on their mortgage.
3. **Buyer who has already sold (subject to contract):** Their chain is progressing, reducing risk.
4. **Buyer who needs to sell:** The most common but riskiest. Their ability to complete depends entirely on finding their own buyer.

**Managing chain risk:**
- Ask your agent to verify the buyer's position before accepting an offer
- Request proof of mortgage agreement in principle
- Stay in regular contact with your solicitor and agent about chain progress
- Consider accepting a slightly lower offer from a chain-free buyer over a higher offer from a buyer in a long chain

### Negotiating Offers

**When an offer comes in:**
1. Your agent is legally required to pass on all offers (in writing, if you request it).
2. Do not respond immediately. Take time to consider.
3. Ask your agent about the buyer's position: chain status, mortgage approval, timeline, motivation.
4. A first offer is rarely a final offer. If it is below your expectation, make a counter-offer.

**Negotiation tips:**
- Know your bottom line before viewings begin
- Consider the whole package, not just the price (a lower cash offer may net you more than a higher offer from a buyer in a chain who might pull out)
- Keep emotion out of it. This is a financial transaction.
- If you have multiple offers, inform all parties. Competition drives prices up.

### Choosing a Solicitor/Conveyancer

Your solicitor or licensed conveyancer handles the legal work of transferring ownership. This is not a place to cut corners.

**What to look for:**
- Accreditation with the Law Society's Conveyancing Quality Scheme (CQS)
- Experience with your type of property (leasehold, listed building, new build, etc.)
- Clear, fixed-fee pricing (expect GBP 850-1,800 + disbursements for a standard sale)
- Responsiveness -- slow conveyancing is the number one cause of delayed completions
- Availability during the period you expect to be selling

**Key tip:** Instruct a solicitor before you list your property. This means they can start preparing the title pack and property information forms immediately once you accept an offer, saving weeks.

### Surveys and What to Expect

Your buyer will almost certainly commission a survey. Understanding the types helps you anticipate issues:

**Mortgage valuation:** A basic check for the lender to confirm the property is worth what they are lending against. Not a detailed survey.

**Homebuyer's Report (RICS Level 2):** The most common survey type. Covers the condition of accessible areas and flags significant issues. Does not involve moving furniture or lifting floorboards.

**Building Survey (RICS Level 3):** The most thorough survey. Used for older properties, properties in poor condition, or those with known issues. Involves a detailed inspection.

**What happens if the survey raises issues:**
- Minor issues are normal and expected. Do not panic.
- Significant issues (subsidence, structural movement, Japanese knotweed, asbestos) will often trigger a renegotiation on price.
- Decide in advance what your position is on renegotiation. Having a bottom line prevents emotional decision-making.
- If you know about issues, disclose them upfront. Surprises in surveys kill deals.

### Exchange and Completion Timeline

**Typical timeline (England and Wales):**
- Offer accepted: Week 0
- Solicitors instructed: Week 0-1
- Searches ordered: Week 1-2
- Searches returned: Week 3-6 (varies significantly by local authority)
- Survey completed: Week 2-4
- Mortgage offer issued: Week 3-6
- Enquiries raised and answered: Week 4-8
- Exchange of contracts: Week 8-12
- Completion: Typically 1-4 weeks after exchange

**Total: 10-16 weeks** from offer accepted to moving day, though it can be faster or slower.

**Exchange of contracts** is the point at which the sale becomes legally binding. Before exchange, either party can pull out without penalty (except for their own legal costs). At exchange, a completion date is fixed and a deposit (typically 10%) is paid by the buyer.

**Completion** is moving day. Funds are transferred, keys are handed over, and ownership changes.

**Scotland is different:** Scotland uses a system of "missives" (a series of formal letters between solicitors). The process is generally faster and more binding at an earlier stage. Once missives are "concluded," the sale is legally binding.

## 7. Legal & Financial Considerations

### EPC Requirements

An Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) is a legal requirement for all properties being sold or let in England and Wales. You must have a valid EPC (less than 10 years old) before marketing your property. The certificate rates your home from A (most efficient) to G (least efficient) and includes recommendations for improvement.

**Cost:** GBP 60-120 depending on property size and location.

**Why it matters beyond compliance:**
- Properties with higher EPC ratings sell for more (research suggests EPC A-C properties achieve 5-14% higher prices than equivalent G-rated homes)
- Buyer awareness of energy costs is increasing, particularly since the energy price rises of 2022-2023
- Some lenders offer preferential mortgage rates for energy-efficient homes

**Quick EPC improvements:**
- Loft insulation (can move you up 1-2 bands)
- Cavity wall insulation
- LED lighting throughout
- Smart thermostat
- Double glazing (if not already fitted)

### Stamp Duty (and How It Affects Your Buyer)

You do not pay Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) when selling, but your buyer does, and it directly affects what they can afford to offer you.

**Current SDLT rates (England and Northern Ireland, from April 2025):**
- Up to GBP 125,000: 0%
- GBP 125,001 to GBP 250,000: 2%
- GBP 250,001 to GBP 925,000: 5%
- GBP 925,001 to GBP 1,500,000: 10%
- Over GBP 1,500,000: 12%

**First-time buyers:** 0% on the first GBP 300,000 (for properties up to GBP 500,000).

**Additional properties:** A 5% surcharge applies on top of standard rates for second homes and buy-to-let properties.

**Why this matters to you as a seller:**
- Properties just above a stamp duty threshold can be harder to sell. A property at GBP 260,000 costs the buyer GBP 3,200 in SDLT, while one at GBP 250,000 costs just GBP 2,500. That GBP 700 difference means some buyers may prefer the lower-priced property.
- First-time buyers have more purchasing power below GBP 300,000 due to the relief. If your property is in this range, first-time buyers are a key audience.
- Investors face higher stamp duty, so if your property is typically a rental (studio, one-bed flat in a city centre), investor demand may be more price-sensitive.

**Note:** Stamp duty rates and thresholds change periodically. Always check the latest rates at gov.uk/stamp-duty-land-tax.

### Capital Gains Tax (If Applicable)

If the property you are selling is your main residence and has been throughout your ownership, you are exempt from Capital Gains Tax (CGT) under Private Residence Relief.

**CGT may apply if:**
- The property was not always your main home (e.g., you rented it out for a period)
- It is a second home or investment property
- You own more than one property and have elected a different one as your main residence
- The property has been let to tenants at any point

**Current CGT rates on residential property (2025-26):**
- Basic rate taxpayers: 18%
- Higher/additional rate taxpayers: 24%

**Annual exempt amount:** GBP 3,000 per person (2025-26).

If CGT applies, consider speaking to a tax adviser before listing. There are reliefs and allowances (Lettings Relief, Private Residence Relief for the final period of ownership) that can significantly reduce the bill.

### Material Information Requirements

From 2022, the National Trading Standards Estate and Letting Agency Team introduced enhanced requirements for "material information" in property listings. Agents must now include detailed information upfront, including:

- Council tax band and annual amount
- Asking price
- Tenure (freehold, leasehold, commonhold)
- Property type and construction materials
- Number of bedrooms, bathrooms, and living rooms
- EPC rating
- Parking availability
- Any flooding risks
- Access to utilities (mains water, gas, electricity, broadband speed)
- Rights of way, restrictions, or covenants
- If leasehold: remaining lease length, ground rent, service charge, and any known issues

**Why this matters to you:** Providing this information upfront speeds up the sale and reduces the risk of buyers pulling out after discovering something they were not told. Be honest and thorough. An informed buyer is far less likely to renegotiate or withdraw than one who feels misled.

## 8. Top 20 Tips Summary

A quick-reference checklist of the most impactful actions you can take:

1. **Research comparable sales** before any agent visits, using Land Registry and Rightmove sold prices.
2. **Get at least three agent valuations** and compare their evidence, not just their figures.
3. **Beware agents who overvalue** -- the highest valuation is not always the best one.
4. **Choose your agent on performance data** (sales in your area, time to sell, prices achieved), not on name recognition alone.
5. **Negotiate the agent's fee** -- they are almost always flexible, especially in competitive markets.
6. **Instruct a solicitor early**, ideally before you go on the market, to avoid delays later.
7. **Get your EPC done first** -- you need it before marketing begins, and a good rating is a selling point.
8. **Declutter ruthlessly** -- buyers need to see the space, not your belongings.
9. **Invest in kerb appeal** -- a clean front door, tidy path, and planted pots cost little but make a strong first impression.
10. **Deep clean the entire property** before photos are taken, including windows, ovens, and bathrooms.
11. **Repaint in neutral colours** if walls are scuffed, bold, or dated.
12. **Fix the small things** -- dripping taps, squeaky doors, and broken tiles signal neglect to buyers.
13. **Insist on professional photography** with a minimum of 15 high-quality images.
